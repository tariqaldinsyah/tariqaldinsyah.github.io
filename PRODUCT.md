# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Potential clients (early-stage startups, design agencies, tech companies), design directors, and hiring managers actively evaluating UI/UX talent. They arrive knowing what good design looks like; they will make a judgment in seconds. Secondary: collaborators and peers who want to understand Tariq's aesthetic POV.

## Product Purpose

Establish Tariq Aldinsyah as a senior UI/UX designer with distinctive aesthetic taste and executional depth. The portfolio's job is not to explain what a UI/UX designer does — it's to demonstrate it through the quality of the portfolio itself. Success is a project inquiry or interview invitation from a context where craft matters.

## Brand Personality

Editorial-decisive. Precise-deliberate. Confident-singular. The physical object: a high-end editorial magazine that happens to be about technology — bold art direction, generous white space, confident type choices, every element placed with purpose. Not the magazine's cover trying to look designed; the feature spread inside where the layout tells you something about the subject.

## Anti-references

- **Framer template portfolio**: Blur-card heroes, purple-to-blue gradient anything, scroll-triggered fade-rise on every section, glassmorphism, neon accent on dark — the aesthetic that signals "I found a template." Avoid any combination that produces this read.
- **Behance showcase grid**: Identical case-study cards in a uniform grid, numbered section eyebrows (01 · About, 02 · Process), project thumbnails in equal-sized boxes — the aesthetic that signals "I exported from Behance." Avoid uniform repetition and numbered scaffolding.
- **Editorial dark portfolio**: White-on-black, display-serif-italic headline, tracked small-caps mono labels on every section, ruled separators, monochromatic restraint — this is the currently-saturated "tasteful designer" lane. The fact that it's tasteful is why it's a trap. Avoid looking like a Klim type specimen.

## Design Principles

1. **Practice what you preach.** A UI/UX designer's portfolio must demonstrate the same hierarchy, restraint, and craft they'd demand in client work. Sloppy spacing, inconsistent rhythm, or visual noise in the portfolio reads as contradiction.
2. **Hierarchy earns its place.** The most important thing on the page must feel like the most important thing. Scale jumps are not decoration; they are information. When everything is the same weight, nothing stands out.
3. **One bold color, committed.** Lime is the signature. Use it as a statement, not a sprinkle. It should feel deliberate every time it appears, not reflexive.
4. **Show, don't announce.** Strong content hierarchy replaces section labels. The reader should know what section they're in from the content's composition, not from a tiny uppercase kicker above every heading.
5. **Density by design.** Some moments are generous (hero, project hero), some are dense (skills, education). The contrast between density levels is itself part of the rhythm.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. Dual theme (dark primary, light available). `prefers-reduced-motion` respected throughout. Minimum 4.5:1 contrast on body text in both themes. No color-only information encoding.
